Theorem 2.4 (Existence of an Eulerian path) A connected graph G has an Eulerian path from vertex v to w (v != w) if and only if w are the only vertices with an odd degree.

Proof. Consider the graph G' obtained by adding the edge (w,v) to G. G' is connected and each vertex has an even degree, so it has an Eulerian cycle (w,v,...,w). Delete the first edge from this cycle to obtain the Eulerian path (v,...,w) in G.

Example 2.1 Looking back at Figure 1.4 (the graph modeling the bridges in Konigsberg), you notice that there are four vertices with an odd degree. It follows that there is neither an Eulerian cycle, nor an Eulerian path.

Definition 2.13 (Subgraph) A graph (V1,E1) is a subgraph of (V,E) if V1 subseteq V and E1 subseteq E.

Definition 2.14 (Component of a graph) A component C of a graph G is a maximal connected subgraph of G, i.e. for any connected subgraph C' with C subset C', we have that C' is not connected.

Theorem 2.5 (Partition of a graph) The components (Vi,Ei) (i = 1,...,n) of a graph (V,E) form a partition , i.e., (V,E) = (Union_{i=1..n} V_i, Union_{i=1..n} E_i), and for any i != j, V_i intersection V_j = 0 and E_i intersection E_j = 0.

Proof. Clearly every vertex and edge belongs to at least one component. Suppose a vertex belongs to two components a and b; then the union of a and b is a connected subgraph of (V,E), so a = b. Since the degree of a vertex and all incident edges belong to the same component, the proof can be completed easily.

Theorem 2.5 is important because it is often easier to prove properties for connected graphs: Theorem 2.5 tells us that a non connected graph can be uniquely divided into connected components.
